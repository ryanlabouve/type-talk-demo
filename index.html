Emblem Book

by George Wither, 1635

A collection of emblemes, ancient and moderne quickened with metrical illustrations

If there had not beene some Bookes conceitedly composed, and sutable to meane capacities, I am doubtfully whether I had even beene to delighted in reading, as thereby to attaine to the little knowledge I have. For, I doe yet remember, things honestly pleasant, brought mee by degrees, to love that which is truely profitable.

This Game occasions not the frequent crime of swearing, or mispending of our time—nor losse of money—for, the play is short, and, ev'ry gamer winneth by the sport… it needs no dice.

And as David said, His Heart lhewed him the wickedness of the ungodly.

Even so, I may truly acknowledge, that mine owne Experience hath shown mee so much of the common ignorance and infirmitie in mine owne person, that, it hath taught mee, how those things may be wrought upon in others, to their best advantage.

To The Reader

Therefore, though I can say no more to disswade from vice or to incourage men to vertue, than hath, already beene said in many learned Authors; yet I may be of occasion by These endeavours, to bring that, the oftner into remembrance.

Viniger, Salt, or common Water make sauces more pleading. Yea, wee have had frequent tiroofes, that a blunt left hath moved to more conftderatton, than a judicious discourse.

I approve and make use of, to those good purposes, according at my leisure, and the measure of my facultie will permit jiltat, Vanilic I might

Wither was employed by a London publisher called Henry Taunton to write English verses to illustrative the beautiful allegorical plates.
